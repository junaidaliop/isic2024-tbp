Methods

From the most basic to the most complex — the metric, the CV spine, the features, the GBDT, the image experts, and the stack

This page is deliberately verbose and bottom-up. We start with the single most important and most misunderstood thing — the metric — and build up to the full hybrid pipeline.

The metric: partial AUC above 80% TPR

Why not accuracy, and why not plain AUC?

At 0.098% prevalence, accuracy is meaningless (predict “benign” always → 99.9% accurate, zero malignant caught). Plain ROC-AUC is better — it is threshold-free and prevalence-robust — but it averages performance over all operating points, including useless low-sensitivity ones. A clinical triage tool only matters where it catches almost every cancer: the high-sensitivity tail.

The definition

The official ISIC-2024 metric is the partial area under the ROC curve, restricted to the region where the true-positive rate (sensitivity) is at least 80%:

\[ \text{pAUC}_{80} \;=\; \int_{\text{TPR}=0.80}^{1.0} \text{TPR}\; d(\text{FPR}) \]

Equivalently: integrate the ROC curve only over the band TPR ∈ [0.80, 1.0] (FPR ≤ 0.20), then report the area in TPR-units × FPR-units. Because the band is 0.20 tall, the score is bounded in [0, 0.20]:

  • random classifier ≈ 0.02,
  • perfect classifier = 0.20.

A move from 0.169 → 0.174 is therefore a +3.2% relative gain on a small absolute scale — small absolute moves are large relative moves, and we phrase improvements that way throughout.

Two pitfalls we pinned down up front

WarningThe 80% vs 88% trap

The public ISIC metrics repository’s README mentions a 88% TPR threshold (which would bound the score in [0, 0.12]), but the live competition used 80%. Mixing the two makes any number incomparable. We pin 80% for every leaderboard-comparable claim, and our scorer is hard-coded to it.

We do not re-implement the metric loosely. src/cv.py:pauc_above_tpr is the only function allowed to compute it, and it is proven numerically identical to the vendored official scorer (src/metric_official.py, © 2024 N. R. Kurtansky, MSKCC) by a unit test (tests/test_cv.py, part of the 9/9 passing suite).

The CV spine: the whole game

In ISIC-2024, leaky cross-validation sent teams ~200 places down on the private split. Validation is not a step here — it is the spine everything hangs on.

  • Patient-grouped, target-stratified folds. We use StratifiedGroupKFold (5 folds, SEED =
    1. with the patient as the group and the malignant label as the stratification target.
    • Grouped → no patient straddles folds (a patient’s lesions are correlated; a single straddling patient leaks).
    • Stratified → each fold holds 77–83 of the 393 positives, so no fold is starved of signal.
  • Frozen once. The split is written to data/folds.parquet and read, unchanged, by every expert and by the stack. There is exactly one split in the entire project.
  • Hard no-leak guarantee + veto. The cv-guardian agent has veto power over anything touching the split or the metric: if a change would leak patients across folds, recompute the metric independently, or compare models on mismatched splits, it must block. This is the anti-shakeup discipline that makes our OOF number trustworthy.

Out-of-fold (OOF) predictions. For each fold we train on the other four and predict on the held-out fold; concatenating the five held-out predictions gives one prediction per row, none of which ever saw its own row in training. We score pAUC once on this full OOF vector. Because pAUC is non-additive across folds, the full-OOF number (0.17430) differs trivially from the mean of per-fold pAUCs (0.17487) — both are reported.

Tabular feature engineering

The predictive signal lives largely in the tabular metadata. src/features.py builds, from ISIC-2024 columns only (no external data, every feature leak-checked):

  • Geometry & size ratios — normalized lesion size, perimeter, area, eccentricity, axis ratios, border composites; raw size is strong, ratios make it scale-robust.
  • Color / hue / luminance contrasts — tbp_lv_H (hue angle), lesion-vs-skin contrast, color uniformity, ΔA/ΔB color deltas. Hue alone reaches univariate AUC 0.81.
  • Border & shape composites — irregularity and asymmetry combinations.
  • 3D position — the lesion’s location on the body surface (TBP gives true 3D coordinates).
  • Patient-relative “ugly-duckling” deviations — the decisive block. For each lesion and each base feature, how anomalous is it relative to the same patient’s other lesions?
    • pdev_* — deviation (signed/standardized distance from the patient’s own mean),
    • prank_* — within-patient percentile rank,
    • pxc_* — patient × category interactions (e.g. hue × body location).
    • These encode the clinical “ugly-duckling sign” directly. They turn out to be ~65% of total GBDT gain (see Results and Ablations).
  • Per-fold smoothed target encoding — for high-cardinality categoricals (e.g. location), computed inside each fold’s training data only and applied to the held-out fold, with smoothing toward the global mean.
ImportantLeak-safety of features

Every patient-relative statistic and every target encoding is computed fold-locally (from training rows only) and applied to held-out rows — so no held-out information ever flows into a feature. The cv-guardian audits this. Patient-relative deviations are safe because they use the patient’s own benign moles, which are in the same fold by construction (patients don’t straddle folds).

The GBDT expert

src/gbdt.py trains the tabular expert. Two stories matter here — one cautionary, one constructive.

The underfit bug (and the fix)

Our first GBDT scored 0.09941 — barely 5× random. The cause was a classic imbalance foot-gun: is_unbalance=True up-weighted the 393 positives ~1000×, and the LightGBM early-stopping was driven by built-in AUC, which under that weighting peaked at best_iter = 1 — the model essentially never trained.

The fix, in two parts:

  1. Drop is_unbalance — let undersampling (below) handle the imbalance instead of extreme instance weights.
  2. Early-stop on the official pAUC — a custom feval in src/gbdt.py evaluates pAUC@80%TPR directly, so early stopping optimizes the metric we are actually scored on.

This single change took the model from 0.09941 → 0.11826 (+0.019). See Ablations → Tabular.

The production recipe

The final tabular expert (greysky-lineage hyperparameters; see Credits) is a bagged ensemble:

  • Manual undersampling to ~1% (neg_ratio = 0.01 → ~100 negatives per positive, ~4k rows per fold/seed) so each booster trains fast and is not drowned by benigns.
  • 5-seed bagging (seeds 12/22/32/42/52) of LightGBM, averaged on rank.
  • CatBoost rank-blend — a second GBDT family bagged the same way; the final tabular OOF is 0.8·rank(LightGBM-bag) + 0.2·rank(CatBoost-bag). CatBoost is the weaker family here, so an equal 0.5 blend dilutes (0.16809); 0.2 is the swept optimum (0.16890).

Result: OOF pAUC 0.16890 at essentially zero inference cost (0.86 M “params” of trees, ~0 GFLOPs, 0.02 ms/img). This is the efficient anchor of the whole frontier.

The image experts

src/vision/* trains the deep protagonist. Each backbone is one point on the efficiency frontier, measured by the efficiency-auditor.

The recipe (shared across backbones)

  • Small, natural-image-pretrained backbones at 128 px (and 224 px for the resolution axis). Pretraining is ImageNet / FCMAE only — no skin-cancer labels, so the no-external-data claim survives (pretrained weights are allowed; external training data is not).
  • Per-epoch negative undersampling to ~1:1 (neg_ratio, pos_mult). Each epoch sees all positives + a fresh random ~equal sample of negatives, so an “epoch” is only a few thousand images and runs in ~4.5 s. A full 5-fold backbone trains in ~8 minutes. Re-sampling the negatives each epoch means the model still sees the whole benign distribution over time.
  • Loss: BCE with label smoothing 0.05 (softens the 393 hard positives).
  • Augmentation: the transV2 policy (flips, rotations, color jitter within reason — classical only, no generative augmentation).
  • EMA(0.995) + mixup(α=0.2). Weight EMA stabilizes the noisy undersampled updates; mixup regularizes. EMA decay matters: at ~23 steps/epoch, decay 0.999 (time-constant ~1000 steps) lags the weights and hurts (fold-0: 0.146 vs 0.156); 0.995 + warmup helps (0.163). Pinned at 0.995.
  • Resolution as a frontier axis. We keep both convnextv2_nano@128 (0.15311) and @224 (0.15945) as distinct points, because resolution trades accuracy for cost.

The 12-backbone frontier sweep

We swept a family spanning the cost axis from ~2.5 M to ~294 M params: mnv4_small, effvit_b0, starnet_s1, fastvit_t8, effnetv2_b0, ghostnetv3, vit_tiny, vit_small, convnextv2_nano (128 px and 224 px), convnextv2_tiny, swinv2_tiny, eva02_small, and the heavy anchor mnv5_300m. The clear lesson (see Results and Ablations): heavy transformers collapse to near-random at 393 positives (SwinV2 @256 → 0.104, EVA-02 @336 → 0.100), while the small convnextv2_nano is the sweet spot.

Self-supervised pretraining — the principled “one-up” (future work)

The cleanest in-constraint way to exploit the 400k+ unlabeled crops — without importing external data or fabricating positives — is self-supervised pretraining (MAE / DINOv2-style) on the crops themselves, then fine-tuning on the 393 labels. This substitutes our own unlabeled data for the external data we banned, and stays fully inside the rules. We present it as the principled next step rather than a completed result; it is the most promising lever we did not exhaust in the 2-day budget.

The stack (combiner)

src/stack.py fuses the experts. We compared three combiners on the same OOF vectors:

Combiner What it does OOF pAUC
Rank-average average the percentile ranks of GBDT and image OOF — param-free 0.17430
Meta-LightGBM a second-level GBDT over the expert OOFs 0.17108
Learned per-lesion gate (MoE) a network picks expert weights per lesion 0.15007

We also tried adding the image embedding (PCA components) and an image-space ugly-duckling feature into the GBDT; both hurt (< 0.1743).

NoteWhy the trivial combiner wins at 393 positives

A rank-average has zero parameters to fit, so it cannot overfit the validation folds. Every learned combiner has to estimate its parameters from the same 393 positives the experts already used — there is simply not enough positive signal left to learn a better fusion than “average the ranks.” The learned gate even falls below the tabular expert alone (0.150 < 0.169). This is the project’s recurring theme, and it is a result, not a disappointment: at this scale, simpler is both cheaper and more accurate. Full numbers in Ablations.

Efficiency as a first-class axis

Every reported model logs params, FLOPs, and single-thread CPU latency (src/efficiency.py) alongside its pAUC, so each model is one point on a quality-vs-cost plane. The Pareto-optimal set — points that no other model beats on both axes — is the headline figure. A model that does not earn its cost does not stay in the pipeline. See Results.
